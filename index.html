the rise of the bots

The rise of bots is inevitable. We already treat them like entities — watch GothamChess commentate Leela vs Stockfish and you'll hear it: we give bots names, respect their brilliance, narrate their rivalries like legends. Culturally, we're ready. We will own them, give them identities, and let them work alongside us. But here's the bigger thing — the efficiency of AI agents will inevitably give rise to robotics. A device with an AI brain isn't a gadget. It's a robot. A phone running an autonomous agent in Termux on a $25 Android is a robot with no legs. That's not metaphor — that's the Darwinian birth of robotics. Hard metal devices with AI brains are the first species.

And the ecosystem is exploding. OpenClaw — the open-source agent framework — hit 189,000 GitHub stars in 80 days. Nearly a million npm installs in a single week. Thousands of developers building agents that read email, manage calendars, write code, control smart homes, and run businesses — all from a terminal. These aren't chatbots. They're persistent, autonomous agents with memory, skills, and souls. The infrastructure for the bot economy is being laid right now, in the open, by people who believe your first employee should be a bot.
